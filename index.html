Mapping New York City POIs

This map compares two distinct datasets of Points of Interest (POIs) in New York City: OpenStreetMap (crowd-sourced) and CommonPlace (government-curated). While OpenStreetMap reflects informal, user-defined spaces, CommonPlace presents an official, institutional perspective on what is publicly significant. By mapping and contrasting the two, this project raises questions about who has the authority to define place, what is considered “official,” and whose spaces are rendered visible in our digital geographies.

About

OpenStreetMap (OSM) is a global, collaborative mapping platform where volunteers contribute geographic data about the world around them. POIs in OSM are created and tagged by everyday users, meaning the dataset reflects informal, hyperlocal knowledge—restaurants, shops, places of worship, and more. This data is dynamic and democratic, but varies in accuracy and completeness depending on who participates and which places are prioritized.

About

CommonPlace is a curated dataset compiled by New York City government agencies, representing officially recognized Points of Interest such as schools, hospitals, libraries, and civic facilities. These locations are often tied to funding, governance, and regulation, offering a more institutional view of public space. While precise and vetted, the dataset reflects top-down definitions of value and may overlook grassroots or community-defined spaces.
